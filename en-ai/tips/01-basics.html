Tetris rotation systems are broadly divided into "Sega-style" and "BPS-style". BPS-style seems to be becoming the world standard recently, called the "World Rule".
TGM follows the Sega-style rotation system, and as shown in the table above, except for the I-piece, pieces rotate such that the height of their lowest edge does not change. You could also say they roll along the ground.
It might seem a bit unnatural at first, but I think this rotation system makes rotation placement (回転入れ - kaiten-ire) easy to use and features easy operation in high-speed drop areas.

About the "Well"

The "Well" (or Playfield) is composed of 20 rows (lines) × 10 columns. Usually counted as 1st row, 2nd row... from the bottom, and 1st column, 2nd column... from the left.
However, as shown in the diagram, pieces can overhang one row above when rotated. To rotate the I-piece, the top 3 rows of the well must be empty, but other blocks can be rotated if the top 2 rows are empty.
The collection of stacked blocks is called the "Stack" (地形 - chikei).
If a block overlaps the stack when it appears, the game is over; this is called "topping out" (窒息 - chissoku).
The block appearing next, displayed above the well, is called the "NEXT display" or simply "NEXT".

About "Level" and "Max Out"

Game progress is represented by "Level (Lv)".
It starts at Lv 0, and clearing the game at Lv 999 is called "maxing out" (カンスト - kansuto), short for counter stop.
The level increases by 1 each time a block appears (tsumo - ツモ), and increases by the number of lines cleared when lines are cleared.
(Meaning, to max out, you drop just over 700 blocks and clear just under 300 lines).

Each 100-level interval is called a "section".
The boundary between sections (Lv *99 → Lv *00, only Lv 998 → Lv 999 at the end) can only be passed by clearing lines.
Just drawing a piece keeps the level at Lv *99; this is called "level stop". This results in lost time during time attacks.

Example: Clearing 1 line with a block at Lv 99 makes it Lv 100, and the next piece draw is Lv 101.
Clearing 4 lines with a block at Lv 98 makes it Lv 102, and the next piece draw is Lv 103.
Clearing 4 lines with a block at Lv 95 makes it Lv 99, and the next piece draw is still Lv 99.

About "20G"

The game draws 60 images per second to express movement, and each of these frames is called a "frame".
Block drop speed is expressed in units of "G", representing "how many rows dropped per frame".
One of the most characteristic features symbolizing the TGM series is 20G, where blocks don't so much fall as suddenly "appear on the ground" (or rather, on the stack).

About "Lock Delay" and "ARE"

There is a slight delay between when a block touches the stack and when it locks (the block flashes white momentarily), during which it can be rotated or moved horizontally.
Especially in 20G where blocks appear grounded instantly, this period is used to slide blocks for movement.
This time is called "Lock Delay" (固定時間 - kotei jikan), and in TGM it is set to about 0.5 seconds. It shortens in the advanced stages of TAP.
Lock delay resets when the piece drops down a step. (See Chapter 5)

There is also a slight delay after a block locks and before the next block appears; this is called "ARE".
It shortens in the advanced stages of TAP.

About "Soft Drop" and "DAS"

Holding the lever down during block fall allows you to make the block fall faster. This speed is 1G; if the natural gravity is faster than that, it doesn't change.
Pressing the lever down while a block is grounded forces the block to lock immediately, even if lock delay time remains.
In the TGM series, "Soft Drop" (下入れ - shita-ire) generally refers to the latter. The former is also called "Drop".

Holding the lever sideways for a while allows for high-speed horizontal movement of the block; this is called "DAS" (Delayed Auto-Shift) (横溜め - yoko-tame).
(The movement speed is equivalent to 1G, so it's the same as inputting sideways every frame).
The time until it "charges" is called "DAS Charge Time" (横溜め時間 - yoko-tame jikan), which is about 0.25 seconds in TGM. It shortens in the advanced stages of TAP.
DAS input is accepted even before the block appears. If it's already "charged" before appearance, the block will fly sideways the moment it appears.
This is important for time attacks.

About "IRS"

IRS stands for Initial Rotation System (先行回転入力システム - senkou kaiten nyuuryoku system).
If a button is held down when the block appears, the block will appear in the well already rotated once.
It enables comfortable controls even at high drop speeds, so you should consciously use it early on to make it natural.

→
Avoiding top out using IRS.
Appearing in the normal state would cause a top out, but IRS allows avoiding the stack shape.
When using IRS would cause a top out, pressing the button is ignored and the piece appears normally. (Corrected 2008/08/26)

About "Wall kicks"

→
Consider rotating a block near the wall as shown.
Thinking according to the rotation rules presented above, it seems impossible to rotate because the left square would clip into the wall.
However, because there is space to shift to the right, the position is automatically corrected, allowing the rotation.
This allows easy rotation of blocks moved to the edge with DAS, and along with IRS, it supports the comfortable controls of the TGM series.
It's useful in many situations, such as the rotation placements (回転入れ - kaiten-ire) introduced in Chapter 8 and the control optimization introduced in Chapter 11.
