In 20G where movement range is limited, maintaining a good stack shape consistently requires not just "fitting pieces into given gaps" but also "actively creating easily stackable shapes".
(The placement method of keeping the 5th column high, introduced in Chapter 6, is the prime example of this.)

Keep Terrain Allowing I-piece to Reach the Right Edge

→
If you stand the T-piece up in columns 8-9, the overall shape becomes "moderately stepped and smooth".
If the drop speed is slow, there's no particular problem, but in 20G, you won't be able to get the I-piece to the right edge.
Therefore, by stopping at columns 7-8 and keeping the right two columns low, you can maintain a stack shape that allows the I-piece to reach the right edge.
→
In this case too, by leaving the right two columns open, you can bring the I-piece to the right edge.
The horizontal hole can be followed up by shifting a J-piece in or using a T-piece rotation placement.
In 20G where "if there's a step, it instantly drops to the bottom", such "shift placements" (ずらし入れ - zurashi-ire) are very easy to use, and with practice, you can slide blocks in with extremely fast operations.
Become able to quickly identify which block is needed.
Shift placements using blocks kept flat like this are also effective during time attacks.
A pattern that creates a shape followable by rotation placement.
T-pieces and Z-pieces are useful here.
Such "hooking placements" (引っ掛ける置き方 - hikkakeru okikata) are quite convenient.
Including those requiring multiple blocks for follow-up, there are countless stacking methods like this.

Key points for the right side of the well are:
・Create horizontal holes opening to the right
・Keep the right 2-3 columns open
If a horizontal hole opens right, it can usually be followed up using shift placements or rotation placements, and if a 2-column width is open, any block can pass through. The concept of "stopping partway" is effective in practice.

It's desirable to know beforehand which block can follow up, but initially, it's not strictly necessary. Rely on feeling, create horizontal holes, try various placements, and find patterns you're good at.

Bringing the I-piece to the Left

Bringing the I-piece left requires a space of 3x2 or more.
The O-piece, often obstructive, is useful here as a base to create a 2-row height.
The operation is "left 1".
Thanks to the Synchro introduced in Chapter 7, gaps in the 2nd column can be jumped over.

Also, leaving the left 3 columns open means making the 4th column high, which means stacking one column left of the 5th column.
Therefore, the "left 1" operation becomes effective.
If the 4th column is 1 step higher than the 2nd, add 1 step. The J-piece stand-up-in-place is effective.
If the 4th column is 1 step lower than the 2nd, add 3 steps. The T-piece "left 1" is effective.

Waiting for a Specific Shape

A pattern waiting for the S-piece on the left edge.
Note also that since the 4th column becomes high the moment the first block is placed, the S-piece can immediately be brought left.
Vertical L-piece insertion (Ｌの縦刺し - L no tate-zashi).
Stack shape with 1-cell bumps is commonly called a "castle" (城 - shiro), and inserting a J or L piece there creates a T-piece rotation setup (left diagram).
Being aware that "a 2x1 gap takes a flat Z or S piece" makes this easier to come up with (right diagram). The pattern of placing a J-piece facing left and waiting for a Z-piece shift placement is very effective.
A 3x2 gap is a chance for a "Denguri-gaeshi" (somersault).
On the left edge, learn the operation "S-piece left 2" as a set.
Depending on the piece flow (tsumo), it's better if you can switch between stopping at left 1 and waiting for L (or O -> J).

Compromise Placement (妥協置き - dakyou-oki)

In 20G where the 5th column is stacked high, holes near the 5th column are inevitably harder to dig out.
Therefore, even when forced to create a hole, the mindset of keeping it as far from the 5th column as possible becomes important.

→
A shape where creating a hole is unavoidable.
One tends to think "let's fill from the bottom rows up", but inserting the Z-piece creates a hole in the 5th column, making recovery difficult.
The vertical thinking of "prioritize filling columns near the 5th" is important.
→
A slightly less obvious pattern of inserting a Z-piece where an S-piece seems like it would fit.
Be careful, as trying to insert an S-piece won't rotate.
→
A pattern of shifting J or L pieces that fill a 3-row height.
→
Inserting the J-piece vertically clears 2 lines, but getting fixated on immediate line clears creates a hole in the 5th column.
If the stack is low, there's the option of discarding that line, but when trying to recover, a hole in the 5th column is fatal. Whatever it takes, fill the 5th column.

If an I-piece comes first, if you can't place it on the right, create a hole and bring it left even if it means doing so. Defending the 5th column is worth that much.
